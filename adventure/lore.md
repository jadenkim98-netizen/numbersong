# Harmonia — A Traveler's Codex

*A short account of the land that sings, the sword that shattered, and the eight keepers who wait for an ear that knows the way home.*

---

## The Shattering of Excalibar

Long ago, Harmonia had one song, and Excalibar was its voice. The blade was forged of all twelve notes hammered around a single home, and while it was whole, no one could get lost — you could stand anywhere, hum anything, and feel exactly how far you were from 1.

Then somebody tried to play the song from the outside. They measured it, note against note, until every tone was defined only by its neighbor and none of them remembered where home was. A song that forgets its home cannot hold itself together. Excalibar rang once, sadly, and came apart into eight pieces.

The pieces did what lost notes always do: they scattered toward whatever felt familiar. The Pommel gem rolled into the sunniest meadow, the Grip sank into the fen, the wings caught in glass branches and cave dark, the blade washed up in three lengths along the harder roads, and the tip flew all the way to the fire on Anvil Peak. In each place, a keeper rose to guard what landed.

The keepers agree on one law: the sword cannot be reforged by hands. Excalibar was made of *heard* notes, so only an ear can remake it — one that can stand in any region, in any key, and still feel where home is. Pass a keeper's trial and the fragment is given freely — they have waited a long time for someone to ask properly.

## Coda

Every traveler in Harmonia is found, sooner or later, by a small companion called Coda. Coda doesn't talk. Coda hums — a note here, a phrase there, always the sound you need a half-second before you need it. The keepers say Coda is the last unbroken sliver of the original song, too small to guard and too stubborn to fade. Coda always hums the home note first. That's not a habit. That's the whole job.

## The Eight Keepers

**Verda, the Meadow Keeper — Staircase Meadows** *(Diatonic — major)*. Verda planted the seven-step meadow herself, one terrace for every note of the major scale, so new travelers could climb a song before they knew they were singing. She walks beside each one, naming the steps by number until home rings true underfoot. In her care lies the **Pommel gem** — the first piece, the stone nearest the hand, because every journey back to the blade begins where you hold on.

**Old Rue of the Fen — Lowmoor Fen** *(Diatonic — minor)*. Rue insists the fen's sadness is only a rumor: nothing in the water ever moved, home just settled down at 6, where the reeds hum it soft. Rest your ear there and the dark turns friendly — and Rue trusts you with the **Grip**, the piece a hand can hold even when it cannot see.

**Sylva of the Glasswood — Glasswood** *(Chords — 1 4 5 6)*. Sylva tends a forest grown entirely of glass, where three notes ring at once and every trunk shows clean through to the one behind it. She teaches travelers to hear a chord the way she sees her wood — not one bright blur, but every voice in its own place. To those who look all the way through, she entrusts the **Left wing** of the crossguard.

**Bassil the Deep — Undertone Caves** *(Chords — 6 2 3 4)*. Bassil has listened to the caves so long he hears the chords daylight forgets — the same friendly notes, gathered dark around 6. Listen deep enough and you carry out the **Right wing** — for no blade is balanced by its bright side alone.

**Marin of the Pillars — Pillar Coast** *(Progressions — major)*. Marin has ferried travelers across the drowned coast since the tide swallowed the old road. Songs cross her water the way her pillars march out to sea: chord leaning on chord, each already tilting back toward home. She keeps the **lower blade**, salt-scoured and waiting for its edge.

**Sable of Sixstone — Sixstone Hollow** *(Progressions — minor)*. In Sable's hollow the dusk never fully lifts, and travelers once swore the sadder stones must lead somewhere else. Sable proved the path only dims; it never strays — every progression still circles home, even when home answers to 6. Sable guards the **mid-blade**, where the sword finds its balance.

**Chroma the Wanderer — Halfstep Crossing** *(Chromatic — major)*. No one knows where Chroma came from; the Wanderer simply arrived one dawn and began painting the empty air between the notes. Where others count seven, Chroma counts twelve — every borrowed color just a note passing through on its way home. The **upper blade** travels with the Wanderer, the length that catches the light first.

**Ferro, the Forgemaster — Anvil Peak** *(Chromatic — minor)*. Ferro has kept the forge burning since the day the blade broke, waiting for ears sharp enough to carry the pieces home. The peak's trial is the hardest: all twelve notes held steady around a home that sits in shadow. Ferro shapes **the tip** — and when it is set, Excalibar sings again, and the whole map wakes with it.

## The Creed of the Keepers

Ask any keeper the secret of their region and you will get the same answer in eight accents: **home never moves.**

Keys change. Octaves change. The light goes grey in the fen and out entirely in the caves; the road climbs, drowns, dims, and splits into twelve. But 1 is always 1, and every note in Harmonia is heard, named, and known by how it stands from home — never by measuring it against its neighbor. Even in the minor lands, home didn't leave; it just lives at 6.

That is why the sword can be reforged at all. The pieces scattered, but the home they were forged around never went anywhere. Find it with your ear, region by region, and the blade remembers the rest.

*Like we never left home.*
